# 0.9 — How to use this playbook (as a team lead)

> **⏱ 10 minutes · 👥 Managers, leads · 🎯 Leaves with:** a simple way to turn the playbook into team behaviour without making it feel like corporate homework.

---

## The job is not "make everyone read it"

If you lead a team, the playbook is not a PDF to forward with *"please go through this by Friday."* That will produce exactly what most training produces: polite acknowledgement, low retention, and no shipped evidence.

Your job is to create a rhythm where people can safely try the work, see visible wins, and earn belts through artefacts the team already values: PRs, recipes, skills, better docs, cleaner handoffs, less ops drag.

The adoption unit is not the chapter. The adoption unit is the week.

---

## Run the first two-week adoption loop

Start with a small loop. Do not announce a transformation.

**Day 1: baseline.** Ask everyone to take [§0.10 — Self-assessment](10-self-assessment.md). Capture where the team actually is: not who is "good at AI," but who has evidence at each belt.

**Day 2: one shared target.** Pick one target for the next two weeks. For a non-coding team, use Ops 101: one workflow saving measurable time. For a product/design team, use White or Yellow readiness: setup health, first PR, or a bug fix.

**Days 3-8: visible attempts.** Put attempts in the open. A stuck setup, a failed prompt, a half-working agent, a draft PR. Public attempts beat private polish because the team learns from the friction.

**Days 9-10: evidence review.** Review what actually shipped. Count artefacts, not optimism. A recipe that saved two hours, a PR under review, a teammate unblocked by a better context file: all count.

Then repeat with a sharper target.

---

## Make champions useful, not heroic

Every team will have one or two people who sprint ahead. Use them carefully.

Do not turn champions into the unofficial support desk. That creates a dependency and quietly punishes the people who learned fastest. Instead, ask champions to produce reusable artefacts:

- a five-minute screen recording of one workflow;
- a short recipe in the team wiki;
- a working prompt with the sensitive details redacted;
- a small skill or checklist others can fork;
- a before/after note showing time saved.

The champion's job is not to answer every question. The champion's job is to leave behind a path.

---

## Protect the safety brief

The fastest way to kill momentum is a sloppy incident. Make [§0.11 — The safety brief](11-safety-brief.md) non-negotiable before anyone uses AI on real Razorpay work.

As lead, model the reflex out loud:

- "Redact the specific customer details; keep the shape."
- "Do this in the approved harness."
- "Money-handling logic needs the higher review bar."
- "If you're not sure, pause and ask in the public support channel."

This is not fear. It is professionalism. In fintech, the guardrails are part of the craft.

---

## What to measure

Track a few things lightly:

- number of people with a completed setup health check;
- number of real PRs opened with AI assistance;
- number of Ops 101 workflows saving measurable time;
- number of reusable recipes, skills, or KB pages contributed;
- number of support questions resolved publicly rather than in DMs.

Do not overbuild the dashboard. A simple tracker is enough until the behaviour is real.

---

## What you should carry forward

- The adoption unit is the week, not the chapter.
- Start with one two-week loop and one shared target.
- Champions should leave artefacts, not become human queues.
- Safety is part of the craft, especially here.
- Measure shipped evidence, not enthusiasm.
- The next chapter, [§0.10 — Self-assessment](10-self-assessment.md), helps each reader choose where to start.
